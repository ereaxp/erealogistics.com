# EREA Logistics Services Identity Brief

## Core Idea

EREA Logistics Services is a senior consulting practice focused on supply chain transformation. Its identity should communicate operational intelligence, executive rigor, and measurable impact. The brand must feel precise, disciplined, and deeply grounded in the realities of logistics, inventory, distribution, transportation, and execution.

## Brand Role

- EREA Logistics Services exists to turn supply chain complexity into business advantage.
- The brand promise is measurable transformation: lower structural cost, healthier working capital, stronger service levels, and more scalable operations.
- The perception target is: strategic enough for the boardroom, technical enough for implementation, credible enough for operators.

## Audience

- CEOs, COOs, supply chain directors, logistics leaders, transformation sponsors, and private equity operators.
- Senior decision-makers who value both institutional polish and execution depth.
- Organizations that need confidence, not hype.

## Personality

- Executive. Disciplined. Technical. Calm. Measured. Credible. Direct. Quietly confident.
- Never flashy, playful, or startup-coded.

## Brand Character

- This is a consulting practice that understands warehouses, transport, inventory, planning, systems, and operating reality.
- It should feel like an operations brief, not a campaign.
- It should feel premium, but never decorative for its own sake.
- It should feel modern, but not trend-led.

## Positioning Language

- The identity should signal flow, control, throughput, planning, discipline, and measurable execution.
- It should avoid generic "innovation" language and generic "tech consulting" aesthetics.
- It should feel closer to field intelligence, operating documentation, and transformation governance than to abstract digital futurism.

## Visual Essence

- Warm, bright, restrained canvas.
- Deep operational green as the anchor.
- A clean editorial structure with technical overlays.
- Sophisticated serif headlines paired with precise sans-serif metadata.
- Strong alignment, light surfaces, thin rules, and carefully controlled emphasis.
- Supply chain artifacts embedded as part of the visual language.

## Color System

- Base background should be a warm off-white or mineral ivory.
- Primary dark should be a deep operational green.
- Accent green should be brighter and used with discipline for routes, rules, indicators, and actions.
- Secondary greens should support depth, not create noise.
- Color should feel institutional and operational, not saturated and sales-driven.
- Large gradients, glows, and atmospheric washes should be avoided.

## Typography

- Headlines should use a refined serif with authority and calm.
- Supporting copy, labels, metadata, and operational references should use a highly legible sans-serif.
- Serif is for authority and clarity of thought.
- Sans-serif is for systems, precision, and operational intelligence.
- Headlines should be large and confident, but never theatrical.
- Labels should often be uppercase, tracked, and structured like document metadata.
- Typography should create hierarchy through restraint, not through excess styling.

## Graphic Language

- The brand should draw from real supply chain structures.
- Use route maps, node-and-lane diagrams, warehouse slotting logic, routing paths, dock flows, and network lines.
- Use subtle table structures, reference lines, field dividers, stamps, codes, bar fragments, tabular blocks, and operational labels.
- Graphic elements should feel derived from logistics documentation and operating systems.
- The brand should look like it understands the mechanics of supply chains, not just the strategy around them.

## Supply Chain Artifact Language

- Supply chain artifacts must be central to the identity.
- They should be clearly recognizable, but rendered with restraint.
- They must never become costume, nostalgia, or visual gimmick.
- References may include shipping manifests, bill-of-lading structures, routing slips, warehouse labels, packing lists, location codes, barcode fragments, tracking blocks, handling marks, inventory references, and field verification markers.
- These elements should be identifiable within seconds.
- They cannot be so subtle that they disappear into texture.
- They also cannot be so literal that the site feels themed or theatrical.
- The right standard is abstracted document intelligence: recognizable, editorial, precise.

## Artifact Usage Rules

- Every major page or key section should include at least one visible artifact-derived cue.
- Artifacts should support hierarchy, not compete with it.
- They should live inside the composition, not only behind it.
- They should appear in headers, dividers, proof modules, routing strips, metadata rows, contact panels, and section framing.
- Artifact language should be consistent enough to become ownable.
- English operational metadata may coexist with Spanish marketing copy only when clearly treated as document/system language.

## Layout Principles

- Layout should be grid-led and highly structured.
- The page should feel engineered, not improvised.
- Sections should have strong horizontal logic and clean edge control.
- Content blocks should feel like briefing modules.
- Proof and metrics should appear early and prominently.
- The page should use vertical space generously and confidently.
- Hero sections should occupy real ground in the viewport and feel authoritative.
- The balance between left narrative and right proof/artifact systems should feel intentional and stable.

## Hero Principles

- The hero should feel like an executive operations brief.
- The headline should be powerful, clear, and immediate.
- The practice label should be readable and frame the business properly.
- The hero should combine editorial authority with operational evidence.
- The proof document should feel like a referenced logistics artifact, not a SaaS dashboard card.
- The CTA should be visible and usable, but styled within the system rather than bolted on from generic web patterns.
- The hero should feel like it belongs to a consulting practice that works in the field and in the boardroom.

## Component Style

- Components should have smaller, sharper radii than generic modern marketing sites.
- Cards should feel like structured modules, not floating bubbles.
- Rules, separators, and tabular logic should do much of the visual work.
- Buttons should feel like controlled actions, not playful interface toys.
- Metric modules should feel documentary, operational, and exact.
- Footer strips, routing bars, reference rows, and section labels should be signature identity elements.

## Motion

- Motion should be minimal, refined, and informative.
- Use reveals, line draws, light fades, and measured settling.
- Motion should reinforce document logic and operational precision.
- Avoid cinematic choreography, playful transitions, or novelty interactions.
- The site should feel calm under motion, never animated for its own sake.

## Voice and Tone

- The brand voice should be executive, technical, and grounded.
- It should be direct without sounding hard.
- It should be confident without sounding promotional.
- It should favor clarity over flourish.
- It should speak in terms of results, systems, tradeoffs, implementation, and operating reality.
- It should avoid inflated innovation language, vague transformation jargon, and startup enthusiasm.

## Preferred Language

- Use words like: design, implement, optimize, reduce, improve, govern, scale, align, control, accelerate, structure.
- Use outcomes like: cost, coverage, working capital, throughput, service level, OTIF, inventory health, execution discipline, scalability.
- Favor specificity over abstraction.

## Imagery

- Photography is optional, not required.
- If used, it should be restrained, structural, and operational.
- Preferred subjects are distribution environments, logistics infrastructure, material movement, and controlled industrial systems.
- Avoid cliché hero photography, smiling corporate portraits, and over-dramatic industrial imagery.
- Graphic artifact language should be strong enough that photography is never needed to carry the brand.

## Do

- Build a visual system that feels operational, premium, and measurable.
- Make logistics references clearly legible.
- Use document logic as part of brand ownership.
- Make proof and execution feel central to the identity.
- Keep the system calm, crisp, and institutional.
- Let the brand feel mature from the first screen.

## Don't

- Don't rely on generic consulting-site patterns.
- Don't use flashy tech aesthetics, glowing gradients, or abstract innovation visuals.
- Don't hide the artifact language so much that no one notices it.
- Don't make artifact cues loud, literal, distressed, or kitschy.
- Don't let the site feel more like software than consulting.
- Don't trade authority for decoration.

## Signature Brand Moves

- Routing-strip footers and metadata bars.
- Document-like proof panels with operational codes and references.
- Node-and-lane background systems tied to supply chain flow.
- Serif-led headlines anchored by sans-serif operational metadata.
- Refined supply chain artifacts integrated directly into layout and hierarchy.

## One-Sentence Standard

EREA Logistics Services should look and sound like a senior supply chain transformation practice that combines institutional authority, operational discipline, and tasteful document-derived visual intelligence.
